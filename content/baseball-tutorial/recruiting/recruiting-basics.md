## How College Recruiting Works

In college baseball, you build your roster through **recruiting** rather than a draft. You compete with other programs to attract high school and junior college players to your school.

## The Recruiting Process

1. **Identify targets** — browse the available recruit pool and scout players
2. **Offer scholarships** — extend scholarship offers to recruits you want
3. **Host visits** — recruits visit your campus and evaluate your program
4. **Compete for commitments** — recruits choose between their offers

:::callout type=info
Recruiting is competitive. Top prospects receive offers from many programs. Your school's prestige, recent success, and available playing time all factor into a recruit's decision.
:::

## What Recruits Consider

When a recruit evaluates your program, they weigh:

- **Scholarship amount** — more money = more attractive
- **Program prestige** — winning programs attract better recruits
- **Playing time** — recruits want to play, not ride the bench
- **Roster fit** — if you already have 3 great shortstops, a SS recruit may go elsewhere
- **Development opportunity** — recruits want to improve and potentially go pro

:::callout type=tip
You don't have to offer the most money to land every recruit. A strong program with a clear path to playing time can beat a richer offer from a crowded roster.
:::

## Scholarship Budget

You have a limited scholarship budget each year. How you allocate it determines the quality and quantity of recruits you can sign:

- **Spread thin** — many partial scholarships, more quantity
- **Concentrate** — fewer full scholarships, higher-quality recruits
- **Balanced** — a mix of partial and full scholarships

:::callout type=warning
Don't blow your entire budget on one recruit. Even the best high schooler isn't guaranteed to be a star. Spread your investment across several good prospects to reduce risk.
:::
