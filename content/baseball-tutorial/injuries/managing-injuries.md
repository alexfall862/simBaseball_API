## Dealing With Injuries

Injuries are inevitable. What matters is how you prepare for them and respond when they happen.

## Prevention

While you can't eliminate injuries, you can reduce their frequency:

1. **Manage stamina** — don't run players into the ground
2. **Use your bench** — rest starters occasionally, especially in blowouts
3. **Watch pitch counts** — high pitch counts increase pitcher injury risk
4. **Carry roster depth** — having backups ready reduces the temptation to overuse starters

:::callout type=tip
The best injury prevention strategy is a deep roster. If you have a quality backup at every position, losing a starter for 3 weeks is manageable. If your bench is empty, one injury creates a domino effect.
:::

## When an Injury Happens

1. **Assess the severity** — check the injury report for expected return date
2. **Make a roster move** — place the player on the IL and call up a replacement
3. **Adjust your lineup** — shift remaining players to cover the gap
4. **Don't panic** — most injuries are short-term

:::callout type=warning
Don't rush players back from injury. A player who returns too early may perform below their baseline and risk reinjury. Let them heal fully unless it's a must-win playoff situation.
:::

## Building Injury Insurance

Strategies for minimizing injury impact:

:::compare
| | Strategy | Cost | Effectiveness |
|---|---|---|---|
| | Deep bench with versatile players | Low | High — covers multiple positions |
| | Extra pitching depth (7-8 relievers) | Moderate | High for pitching injuries |
| | Minor league depth | Low | Good for longer absences |
| | Platoon players | Moderate | Reduces individual workload |
:::

## IL Management

When a player is on the IL:

- Their roster spot opens up for a replacement
- They don't accumulate game stats
- Their stamina recovers during their absence
- They return at the expected date (barring setbacks)

:::callout type=info
Players returning from the IL often have full stamina since they've been resting. They may actually perform better than your fatigued starters in the short term. Plan your lineup to take advantage of this.
:::

:::detail title="Advanced: Multi-injury tracking"
The sim tracks multiple concurrent injuries per player. A player can have a shoulder injury and an oblique strain at the same time. The IL stint lasts as long as the worst injury, and both malus effects stack during recovery.

This is another reason to rest fatigued players — a tired player is more likely to suffer an injury, and playing through a minor injury increases the chance of a second, more serious one.
:::
