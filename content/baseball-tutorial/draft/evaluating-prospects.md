## Reading Scouting Reports

Before the draft, you can scout amateur players to learn about their abilities. Scouting reports show:

- **Current ratings** — where they are now on the 20-80 scale
- **Potential ratings** — their projected ceiling
- **Position** — what position they play
- **Age** — younger players have more development time

:::callout type=info
Scouting isn't perfect. The ratings you see are estimates — the actual player may be slightly better or worse than reported. This uncertainty is part of the challenge.
:::

## What to Look For

The best prospects share these traits:

1. **High potential ceiling** — 65+ potential in key attributes for their position
2. **Youth** — younger players have more time to develop
3. **Premium position** — SS, C, CF, and SP are the hardest positions to fill
4. **Balanced tools** — a player with 55/55/55 tools is often better than one with 75/35/45

:::player-example
position: SS
attributes:
  contact: 40
  power: 35
  eye: 45
  speed: 60
  fielding: 55
  arm: 50
  overall: 42
  potential: 67
caption: "A raw high school shortstop. The current ratings are below average, but the 67 potential ceiling means this player could develop into an above-average starter. The speed and fielding give him a foundation to build on."
:::

## High School vs. College

:::compare
| | | High School | College |
|---|---|---|---|
| | Age | 18 | 21-22 |
| | Current Ratings | Lower | Higher |
| | Potential Ceiling | Higher (usually) | Lower (usually) |
| | Development Time | 4-6 years | 2-3 years |
| | Risk | High | Lower |
| | Reward | Higher ceiling | Safer floor |
:::

:::callout type=tip
If you're rebuilding and can wait, lean toward high school talent for the higher ceilings. If you're close to contending and need help soon, college players are safer picks that arrive faster.
:::

## Red Flags in Prospects

Watch out for:

- **Low potential at key attributes** — a hitter with 45 potential Contact will never be a consistent bat
- **Old for their level** — a 22-year-old in rookie ball is behind the development curve
- **One-tool players** — elite in one area but terrible everywhere else tend to have narrow value
- **Low durability** — injury-prone prospects may never stay healthy enough to develop

:::detail title="Advanced: Scouting accuracy and fog of war"
The accuracy of your scouting reports depends on your organization's scouting investment. You may not see exact ratings for players — the numbers you see could be off by several points in either direction.

Multiple scouting looks at the same player improve accuracy. High-profile prospects at the top of the draft are better scouted than late-round flyers, so there's more uncertainty in the later rounds.
:::
