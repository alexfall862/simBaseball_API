## How the Sim Evaluates Players

Every player in the sim has a **trade value** that the AI uses when evaluating proposals. Understanding this helps you make better offers.

## The Value Factors

:::compare
| | Factor | Weight | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| | Current WAR | High | How productive the player is right now |
| | Potential | High (young players) | Ceiling rating for prospects and young players |
| | Age | Moderate | Younger = more value; older = less |
| | Contract | Moderate | Cheap contracts = more value; expensive = less |
| | Position scarcity | Low-Moderate | Premium positions are harder to fill |
| | Control years | Moderate | More years before free agency = more value |
:::

## Age vs. Production Curves

Player value follows a predictable curve:

- **18-22**: Value is almost entirely potential. Low current production but high upside
- **23-27**: Peak value zone. Strong production + years of control remaining
- **28-31**: Still productive but declining future value
- **32+**: Diminishing value unless still producing at a high level

:::callout type=info
A 24-year-old with 3 WAR and 4 years of control is worth far more than a 32-year-old with 3 WAR and 1 year of control, even though their current production is identical.
:::

## Surplus Value

The key concept in trade evaluation is **surplus value** — the gap between what a player produces and what they cost.

- A player earning $2M who produces $20M worth of WAR has $18M in surplus value
- A player earning $25M who produces $20M worth of WAR has negative surplus value
- Prospects in their pre-arb years have enormous surplus value because they're paid near minimum

:::callout type=tip
When evaluating trades, think in terms of surplus value. Trading a cheap, productive player for an expensive one — even if the expensive player is slightly better — often makes your team worse overall because you lose budget flexibility.
:::

## Prospect Valuation

Prospects are the wild card in trades. Their value depends on:

- **Potential ceiling** — what they could become at their best
- **Probability of reaching it** — not every prospect develops
- **Time to the majors** — a prospect 1 year away is worth more than one 4 years away
- **Position** — premium position prospects (SS, C, SP) carry more value

:::detail title="Advanced: Why prospect value is often overestimated"
Most prospects don't become stars. Even top-100 prospects have roughly a 40-50% chance of becoming average-or-better major leaguers. A bird in the hand (a proven major leaguer) is often worth more than two in the bush (two unproven prospects).

That said, the very best prospects — top-10 types with 70+ potential — are worth more than most available veterans because of the combination of high ceiling and 6+ years of cheap control.
:::
